## 3. REGULATION

### Key Regulations

| Regulation | Scope |
|-----------|-------|
| **Resolucion 3929 de 2013** (MinSalud) | Main technical regulation for fruits and fruit pulp products. Establishes sanitary requirements for processing, packaging, transport, import, and commercialization of fruit juices, pulps, nectars, and concentrates |
| **NTC 5468** (ICONTEC, 2012, reapproved 2022) | Technical standard for juices (zumos), pulps, fruit nectars and concentrates. Includes microbiological requirements, packaging/labeling specs |
| **Decreto 3075 de 1997** | Good Manufacturing Practices (BPM) for food |
| **Resolucion 2674 de 2013** | Updates Decree 3075. Current BPM framework effective July 2014 |
| **Resolucion 719 de 2015** | Risk classification of food products (high, medium, low) |

### INVIMA Sanitary Registration

**Risk classification for fruit pulp:**
- Fruit pulp is generally classified as **medium-to-high risk** depending on processing method
- **High risk (RSA - Registro Sanitario de Alimentos):** Valid for 5 years
- **Medium risk (PSA - Permiso Sanitario de Alimentos):** Valid for 7 years

**Required documentation:**
1. Applicant identification and contact info
2. Product name and brand(s)
3. Composition and nutritional table
4. Modality: manufacture, sell, package, or import
5. Packaging material type
6. Storage conditions
7. Shelf life declaration

**INVIMA Tariff (2025):**
- UVB (Unidad de Valor Basico) for 2025 = COP $11,552
- Tariffs increased 5% from 2024
- Specific registration cost depends on product risk category (calculated in UVBs)
- Consult the official Manual Tarifario at invima.gov.co for exact fees per category

**BPM Requirements:**
- Written Sanitation Plan covering: cleaning/disinfection, solid waste, pest control, potable water supply
- Minimum 10 hours/year training for food handlers
- HACCP certification recommended for export

**Key Definition (Res. 3929):**
> "Pulpa: Producto obtenido por la maceracion, trituracion o desmenuzado y el tamizado o no de la parte comestible de las frutas frescas, sanas, maduras y limpias."

### Margin Estimates
- **General food industry healthy margin:** >10% net margin considered healthy in Colombia
- **Wholesale strategy:** Buy fruit in bulk directly from farmers during peak harvest to maximize margins (prices can drop 30-50% during glut seasons)
- **Value multiplication via processing:** Fresh mango at COP ~$1,500-3,000/kg transforms to retail pulp at COP $13,960-$20,440/kg = **5-10x value multiplication** at retail
- **Export premium:** Processed fruit pulp commands 1.8-2.2x the price of fresh fruit (FOB)
- **Despulpadora ROI:** 1-3 months payback at volume

### Key Cost Components
- **Raw material (fruit):** 30-50% of COGS (highly seasonal)
- **Packaging:** Significant cost; options include bags, pouches, aseptic tetra-style containers
- **Cold chain / energy:** Major ongoing operational cost
- **Labor:** Minimum wage COP ~$1,423,500/month (2026) + social charges (~52%)
- **INVIMA registration:** Based on UVB ($11,552 in 2025); specific cost per product category
- **Rent / facility:** Varies greatly by region
- **Transport / distribution:** Cold chain logistics

### Profitability Drivers
1. **Buying strategy:** Purchase fruit during peak harvest at lowest prices; buy in volume directly from farmers
2. **Product diversification:** Multiple fruit types smooth out seasonal supply/price volatility
3. **Export orientation:** Higher margins in international markets (especially US, Europe)
4. **Vertical integration:** Own or partner with farms (Nutrium model: 81% small producer suppliers)
5. **Scale:** Economies of scale dramatically reduce unit costs
6. **Presentation variety:** Personal (100-250g), family (500g), institutional (1000g+), industrial (drums)

## 9. PASTEURIZED (SHELF-STABLE) PULP — DEEP DIVE

> **Critical distinction:** Most fruit pulp in Colombia is sold FROZEN. Pasteurized shelf-stable pulp (no refrigeration needed) is a different product category with fundamentally different economics, logistics, and competitive dynamics. This section covers that specific opportunity.

### 9.1 Pasteurized vs. Frozen Pulp — Key Differences

| Attribute | Frozen Pulp | Pasteurized Shelf-Stable Pulp |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| **Storage temp** | -18°C (requires freezer) | Ambient, up to 24°C (75°F) |
| **Shelf life** | 12-24 months (if frozen continuously) | 6-24 months at room temperature (depends on packaging) |
| **Cold chain required?** | YES — from production to consumer | NO — stored/shipped like any dry good |
| **Thawing needed?** | YES — must plan ahead | NO — ready to use immediately |
| **Energy cost (storage)** | HIGH — continuous refrigeration | MINIMAL — ambient warehouse |
| **Distribution reach** | Limited to cold chain infrastructure | Anywhere — rural areas, tiendas de barrio, export without reefer containers |
| **Flavor profile** | Closer to fresh (less heat exposure) | Slight cooked notes possible; well-managed HTST/UHT preserves quality well |
| **Nutritional impact** | Minimal loss (cold preserves vitamins) | Some vitamin C loss from heat; other nutrients preserved well |
| **Logistics cost** | 25-35% of total expenses (cold chain in Colombia) | Standard dry logistics (fraction of cold chain cost) |
| **Packaging cost** | Simple polyethylene bags (cheap) | Aseptic bags, doypack, Tetra Pak — more expensive per unit |
| **Capital investment** | Lower (freezer + basic packaging) | Higher (pasteurizer + aseptic filler required) |

**Key research finding:** Heat treatment did NOT significantly affect sensory and physicochemical characteristics of pasteurized pulps vs. fresh pulp, EXCEPT for ascorbic acid (vitamin C) content, which decreases. (Source: Wiley Online Library — Janzantti 2014, study on passion fruit pulp.)

### 9.2 Pasteurization Methods for Fruit Pulp

#### HTST (High Temperature, Short Time)
- **Temperature:** 72-85°C for 15-30 seconds
- **Best for:** Low-viscosity products (juices, diluted pulps)
- **Equipment:** Plate heat exchangers or tubular pasteurizers
- **Result:** Extends shelf life to weeks/months (must combine with refrigeration or aseptic packaging for ambient storage)
- **Pros:** Good balance of safety, speed, cost; preserves more flavor than UHT
- **Cons:** Alone, doesn't enable full ambient storage — needs aseptic packaging or refrigeration

#### UHT (Ultra High Temperature)
- **Temperature:** 135-150°C for 2-5 seconds, followed by immediate cooling
- **Best for:** Products that need true ambient shelf life (like UHT milk)
- **Equipment:** Tubular or plate heat exchangers + aseptic filling system
- **Result:** Commercially sterile product; 12-24 months at ambient temperature
- **Pros:** Longest shelf life; no refrigeration needed at all
- **Cons:** Higher capital cost; potential for slight flavor changes ("cooked" notes)

#### Aseptic Processing (Gold Standard for Shelf-Stable Pulp)
- **Process:** Product sterilized separately (typically UHT), then filled into pre-sterilized packaging in a sterile environment
- **Temperature:** Product heated to ~90-110°C for seconds, cooled to 25-35°C before filling
- **Specific parameters for tropical fruits (research data):**
  - Mango puree: 85°C/15 sec, 85°C/60 sec, 90°C/60 sec, 95°C/15 sec, 95°C/60 sec — all microbiologically sound with no residual enzyme activity
  - Commercial practice: heated to 110°C for 2-3 seconds, cooled, aseptically filled
  - Alternative: heated to 90°C for 1 minute, cooled to 35°C, then filled
- **Result:** Shelf life of 18-24 months at ambient temperature (15-30°C)
- **Pros:** Best quality preservation; industry gold standard; export-friendly
- **Cons:** Most expensive equipment; requires skilled operators; sterility protocols are demanding

#### Batch Pasteurization (VAT / Low Temperature)
- **Temperature:** 63°C for 30 minutes
- **Best for:** Small-scale artisanal operations
- **Equipment:** Simple jacketed kettles (marmitas)
- **Result:** Shorter shelf life; typically requires refrigeration
- **Pros:** Cheapest equipment; simplest to operate
- **Cons:** Long processing time; limited shelf life extension; not viable for ambient-stable product

### 9.7 Distribution Advantage of Shelf-Stable Pulp

#### Cold Chain Problem in Colombia

- **Cold chain logistics costs = 25-35% of total logistics expenses** in Colombia
- **Energy costs for cold storage = 25-30% of operational expenses** (Bogota, Medellin)
- **Colombia cold chain market size:** ~USD $2.3 billion
- **Food loss due to inadequate cold chain:** Up to 40% of perishable goods lost, of which 20-25% is due to cold chain logistics failures (postharvest + transit)
- **Infrastructure gap:** Many rural areas and small towns lack reliable cold chain infrastructure

#### Shelf-Stable Advantage — Quantified

| Factor | Frozen Pulp | Shelf-Stable Pasteurized Pulp | Advantage |
|--------|-------------|-------------------------------|-----------|
| **Transport** | Refrigerated truck required | Standard dry truck | 30-50% lower transport cost |
| **Warehousing** | Industrial freezer (-18°C) | Standard ambient warehouse | 60-80% lower storage cost |
| **Distribution reach** | Limited to cities with cold chain | ANY location in Colombia | Massively expanded market |
| **Tiendas de barrio** | Almost impossible (no freezer) | Perfect fit (shelf product) | Access to 500,000+ tiendas |
| **Export shipping** | Reefer container (~2x cost) | Standard dry container | ~50% lower shipping cost |
| **Spoilage/waste** | High risk if chain breaks | Minimal risk | Lower loss rate |
| **Power outage risk** | Product lost if freezer fails | Zero risk | Business continuity |
| **Retailer adoption** | Needs freezer space (limited, expensive) | Shelf space (abundant, cheap) | Easier retailer onboarding |

#### Strategic Implications
1. **Tiendas de barrio access:** Colombia has ~500,000+ neighborhood stores. Most have NO freezer. Shelf-stable pulp can reach this massive channel that frozen pulp CANNOT.
2. **Rural Colombia:** Poor cold chain infrastructure in rural areas. Shelf-stable pulp can serve these markets.
3. **Export simplification:** No reefer containers needed. Customs cleared faster. Transit time less risky. International shipping becomes dramatically simpler and cheaper.
4. **Disaster/emergency resilience:** Product survives power outages, equipment failures.
5. **Reduced capital for distributors:** Distributors don't need refrigerated trucks or cold storage, lowering barriers to becoming a distributor.

### 9.9 Regulatory Considerations for Pasteurized Pulp

#### INVIMA (Colombia)
- **Resolución 3929 de 2013** (MinSalud): Main technical regulation covering all fruit pulp products including pasteurized
- **Definition:** "Pulpa: Producto obtenido por la maceración, trituración o desmenuzado y el tamizado o no de la parte comestible de las frutas frescas, sanas, maduras y limpias"
- Pasteurized pulp must comply with microbiological limits specified in the resolution's tables
- Labeling must include: ingredients, expiration date, manufacturing date, storage conditions, net weight
- INVIMA registration required; risk classification depends on processing method

#### Key Compliance Points for Pasteurized/Aseptic
- Must demonstrate commercial sterility for ambient-stable claims
- Shelf life validation studies required
- Packaging material must meet food-contact safety standards
- BPM (Buenas Prácticas de Manufactura) certification mandatory
- HACCP recommended (required for export)
- For export: additional certifications (ISO 9001, Global GAP for sourcing) typically expected

## SUMMARY: KEY TAKEAWAYS

### General Market
1. **Market is large and growing:** Colombia's tropical fruit pulp concentrate market is ~USD $447.5M with 4.8% CAGR. The global fruit pulp market is projected to triple by 2035.

2. **Fragmented competition with room to enter:** 34,000+ registered companies, but most are small. Nutrium (Ardila Lulle) dominates industrially. There is space for differentiated, quality-focused entrants.

3. **Regulation is manageable:** Resolucion 3929/2013 + NTC 5468 + INVIMA registration. Clear path but requires investment in BPM compliance and certification.

4. **Entry investment can be modest:** A small plant with 1 MT/hour capacity can be set up for ~COP $283M (~USD $65K). A micro/artisanal operation can start with COP $15-50M.

5. **Raw materials are abundant and relatively cheap:** Colombia's tropical fruit diversity is a major advantage. Direct farmer sourcing (especially during harvest peaks) dramatically reduces costs.

6. **Distribution is multi-channel:** Supermarkets (frozen section), tiendas de barrio, Rappi/delivery apps, institutional (HoReCa), and export are all viable channels.

7. **Export is the highest-margin play:** Processed fruit commands 1.8-5.5x fresh fruit prices. Colombia has duty-free access to Europe for mango puree. US is the #1 destination. Mango, maracuya, and guanabana pulp are the most exportable products.

8. **Value multiplication is significant:** Raw mango at ~COP $2,000/kg becomes retail pulp at COP $15,000-$20,000/kg, representing substantial gross margins before operating costs.

### Pasteurized / Shelf-Stable Specific

9. **Pasteurized shelf-stable pulp is an UNDERSERVED niche in Colombia.** The vast majority of competitors sell frozen. Only a handful of companies (Alimentos SAS, Frutysabor, Ecorganicos, Nutrium's aseptic line) offer ambient-stable product. Alimentos SAS was the FIRST to do it in 2012 — the category is still young.

10. **Elimination of cold chain is a game-changer for distribution.** Cold chain costs 25-35% of logistics expenses in Colombia. Shelf-stable pulp can reach ~500,000 tiendas de barrio (most have no freezer), rural areas, and export markets via standard dry containers.

11. **Capital investment for aseptic is HIGHER than frozen.** Entry-level aseptic filling systems: USD $150,000-$250,000. Complete turnkey aseptic juice/pulp line: USD $120,000-$180,000. HTST pasteurizer alone: USD $3,000-$150,000 depending on capacity. This is significantly more than a frozen pulp operation.

12. **Shelf life of 6-24 months at ambient temperature** depending on packaging. Aseptic bag-in-box achieves 18-24 months. Doypack pouches achieve 6-12 months. Tetra Pak achieves 12-18 months.

13. **For export, aseptic is clearly superior.** No reefer containers (saves ~50% on shipping), no temperature concerns during 30-60 day ocean transit, simpler customs, broader importer base. Nutrium already exports aseptically in dry containers.

14. **Sensory quality is preserved.** Research shows pasteurization does NOT significantly affect taste or physicochemical characteristics vs. fresh, except for vitamin C reduction. Well-managed HTST/UHT maintains excellent flavor.

15. **The strategic play: pasteurized shelf-stable pulp positions you in a different competitive category** than the 34,000 frozen-pulp players. Fewer competitors, better distribution economics, export-ready format, and access to channels (tiendas, rural, non-cold-chain export) that frozen cannot reach.
